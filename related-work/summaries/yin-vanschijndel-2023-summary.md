# Linguistic Compression in Single-Sentence Human-Written Summaries

**Authors:** Fangcong Yin, Marten van Schijndel
**Year:** 2023
**Venue:** Findings of EMNLP 2023
**DOI:** 10.18653/v1/2023.findings-emnlp.532
**URL:** https://aclanthology.org/2023.findings-emnlp.532/

## Summary

This corpus study examines how humans compress information when writing single-sentence summaries. The researchers analyzed summaries across multiple genres and discovered surprising patterns in linguistic compression.

## Key Findings

1. **Morphological Expansion**: Contrary to expectations, summaries feature morphological expansion rather than reduction, suggesting that writers add grammatical complexity even when compressing content.

2. **Increased Lexical Diversity**: Summaries show increased lexical diversity compared to source texts, indicating that writers actively choose varied vocabulary during compression.

3. **Positional Arrangements**: The study found that summaries maintain similar positional arrangements of specific words relative to source texts.

4. **Misalignment between Writers and Readers**: A critical finding is that summary writers employ linguistic strategies that readers prefer, yet their lexical and word-specificity choices don't necessarily match reader preferences. This suggests a disconnect between production and comprehension preferences.

## Relevance to Headline Analysis

This work is highly relevant to the study of news headlines as a reduced register because:
- Headlines represent single-sentence summaries with extreme space constraints
- The findings on morphological expansion challenge assumptions about register reduction
- Understanding writer vs. reader preferences is crucial for automatic headline generation
- The multi-genre approach provides insights into how compression varies across text types

## Methodology

The study uses corpus analysis of human-written single-sentence summaries, examining morphological features, lexical diversity metrics, and word positioning. Human evaluation was conducted to assess reader preferences.
