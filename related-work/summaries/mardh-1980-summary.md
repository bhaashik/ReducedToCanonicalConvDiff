# Headlinese: On the Grammar of English Front Page Headlines

**Author:** Ingrid Mårdh
**Year:** 1980
**Publisher:** CWK Gleerup, Lund
**Series:** Lund Studies in English, Volume 58
**Pages:** 200

## Summary

This is the foundational work on "headlinese" - the term and the systematic linguistic study of newspaper headline grammar. Mårdh's monograph established headline language as a legitimate object of linguistic inquiry and documented its grammatical features systematically.

## Historical Significance

### Origin of "Headlinese"

The term "headlinese" first appeared in 1933 in a journalistic handbook by New York Times journalists R. E. Garst and T. M. Bernstein titled "Headlines and Deadlines." Originally used pejoratively to designate "strange speech that corrupts good English," Mårdh reclaimed and desemanticized the term "to denote simply the language used in headlines" without negative connotations.

### Definition Challenges

Mårdh noted: "No unambiguous definition of 'headline' is known to exist. The word headline seems to be used intuitively, also in linguistic studies of headlines." This observation remains relevant in computational approaches to headline analysis.

## Key Findings

### Grammatical Features of Headlinese

1. **Abbreviated Form**: Headlines use a compressed telegraphic style due to space constraints
2. **Special Syntactic Conventions**: Systematic grammatical patterns distinct from standard written English
3. **Determiner Deletion**: Articles (the, a/an) are systematically omitted
4. **Verb Forms**: Distinctive patterns in tense and aspect usage
5. **Ellipsis**: Systematic omission of elements inferable from context

### Space Constraints Motivation

While acknowledging that space limitations motivated many headline features, Mårdh's analysis goes beyond simple economization to document systematic grammatical patterns that constitute a coherent linguistic variety.

## Methodology

Mårdh's approach combined:
- Corpus analysis of English newspaper front-page headlines
- Systematic grammatical description
- Comparison with Standard English norms
- Documentation of patterns across different newspapers

## Influence and Citations

With 88 citations (Semantic Scholar), this work has been foundational for:
- All subsequent linguistic studies of headlines
- Computational headline generation research
- Register variation studies that include news discourse
- Pragmatic analyses of news language

## Relevance to Current Research

Mårdh's work provides:

1. **Historical Foundation**: Establishes headlines as a legitimate linguistic register worthy of systematic study

2. **Grammatical Baseline**: Documents the core syntactic features that later computational work must account for

3. **Theoretical Framework**: Positions headlines as a grammatical variety, not merely abbreviated text

4. **Methodological Model**: Demonstrates how corpus-based description can reveal systematic patterns in seemingly irregular data

## Limitations

As a 1980 publication:
- Pre-dates digital corpora and computational analysis
- Focuses on print newspapers (pre-web era)
- Limited to English (no cross-linguistic comparison)
- Descriptive rather than computational/formal

However, these limitations don't diminish its foundational value - modern computational approaches build on Mårdh's grammatical insights.

## Connection to Morphosyntactic Analysis

Mårdh's observations about systematic grammatical patterns anticipate modern morphosyntactic analyses:
- Tense transformations (present for past)
- Determiner deletion (morphological feature changes)
- Ellipsis patterns (constituent deletion)
- Verb form variations (finite/non-finite alternations)

The current research on morphosyntactic transformations between canonical and reduced registers validates and extends Mårdh's original insights using computational methods and large-scale corpus analysis.
